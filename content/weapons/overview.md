# Weapons & Systems – The Engineering of Damage

Understanding how weapons and systems work is fundamental to effective gameplay. This guide explains the engineering principles behind damage, penetration, and weapon effectiveness in qualitative terms that remain relevant across patches.

## 1. How Damage Works (Conceptual)

Damage in War Thunder is a complex system involving penetration, post-penetration effects, and module/crew damage. Understanding these concepts helps you make better tactical decisions.

### Penetration

**What It Means**: Penetration is the ability of a projectile to pass through armor. It's not binary—either you penetrate or you don't. There are degrees of penetration effectiveness.

**Effective Penetration**: When a shell penetrates armor effectively, it maintains enough energy to cause damage inside the vehicle. Marginal penetrations may not cause significant damage.

**Factors Affecting Penetration**:
- Shell type (kinetic vs chemical)
- Impact angle
- Armor thickness and type
- Range (penetration decreases with distance)
- Shell velocity

> **Technical Insight:** Penetration is about energy transfer. A shell that penetrates but loses all energy does little damage.

### Post-Penetration Damage

**Spall**: When a shell penetrates armor, it creates fragments (spall) that travel through the vehicle. These fragments damage crew and modules.

**Overpressure**: High-explosive shells create pressure waves that can damage crew and modules even without direct hits on critical components.

**Crew vs Module Damage**: 
- **Crew Damage**: Injures or kills crew members, disabling vehicle functions
- **Module Damage**: Damages vehicle systems (gun, engine, tracks, etc.)

**Why Shot Placement Matters**: Hitting critical areas (crew compartments, ammo racks, modules) causes more damage than random hits. Precision beats raw penetration power.

> **Pro Tip:** Aim for crew and modules, not just penetration. A well-placed shot with moderate penetration beats a poorly-placed shot with high penetration.

### Crew Damage Mechanics

**Crew Functions**: Each crew member has a function:
- **Gunner**: Controls aiming and firing
- **Driver**: Controls movement
- **Loader**: Controls reload speed
- **Commander**: Provides bonuses and can take over other roles

**Crew Elimination**: When crew members are injured or killed, their functions are disabled. A dead gunner means you can't fire. A dead driver means you can't move.

**Crew Protection**: Crew members can be protected by:
- Armor positioning
- Spaced armor
- Crew positioning within the vehicle
- Crew vitality skills

> **Warning:** Crew damage is often more important than module damage. A vehicle with damaged modules but alive crew can still fight. A vehicle with dead crew cannot.

### Module Damage Mechanics

**Critical Modules**:
- **Gun/Breech**: Disables firing ability
- **Engine**: Reduces or eliminates mobility
- **Transmission**: Affects mobility and turning
- **Tracks**: Immobilizes the vehicle
- **Ammo Rack**: Can cause instant destruction if hit

**Module Repair**: Damaged modules can be repaired, but this takes time and makes you vulnerable. Repair positioning is crucial.

**Module Targeting**: Disabling enemy modules before killing them is often safer. Disable their gun, then take your time to finish them.

## 2. Shell Types – Comparative Overview

Different shell types work differently. Understanding these differences helps you choose the right ammunition for each situation.

### AP (Armor-Piercing)

**How It Works**: Solid shot that relies on kinetic energy to penetrate armor.

**Range Behavior**: Penetration decreases with distance due to velocity loss.

**Angle Sensitivity**: Very sensitive to angles. Angled armor significantly reduces effectiveness.

**Ideal Targets**: Flat armor, close to medium range, lightly armored vehicles.

**Common Mistakes**:
- Using against heavily angled armor
- Engaging at extreme ranges
- Expecting high post-penetration damage

> **Technical Insight:** AP is simple but limited. It requires good shot placement to be effective.

### APHE (Armor-Piercing High-Explosive)

**How It Works**: AP shell with explosive filler. Penetrates armor, then explodes inside.

**Range Behavior**: Similar to AP, but post-penetration damage is more consistent.

**Angle Sensitivity**: Sensitive to angles, but explosive filler helps with damage.

**Ideal Targets**: Medium armor, crew compartments, most targets at mid-tier.

**Common Mistakes**:
- Using against heavily angled armor
- Engaging at extreme ranges
- Not aiming for crew compartments

> **Pro Tip:** APHE is the workhorse of mid-tier. It's forgiving and effective against most targets.

### APCR (Armor-Piercing Composite Rigid)

**How It Works**: High-velocity solid shot with very hard core. Excellent penetration at close range.

**Range Behavior**: Penetration drops significantly with distance. Very effective close, poor at long range.

**Angle Sensitivity**: Extremely sensitive to angles. Angled armor dramatically reduces effectiveness.

**Ideal Targets**: Flat armor at close range, heavily armored vehicles frontally.

**Common Mistakes**:
- Using at long range
- Using against angled armor
- Expecting good post-penetration damage

> **Warning:** APCR is situational. It's excellent in specific circumstances but poor in others.

### APDS (Armor-Piercing Discarding Sabot)

**How It Works**: Sub-caliber projectile with sabot that falls away. High velocity, good penetration.

**Range Behavior**: Better range performance than APCR, but still loses penetration with distance.

**Angle Sensitivity**: Less sensitive to angles than APCR, but still affected.

**Ideal Targets**: Heavily armored vehicles, medium to long range.

**Common Mistakes**:
- Expecting high post-penetration damage
- Using against light vehicles unnecessarily
- Not aiming for critical areas

> **Technical Insight:** APDS offers good penetration but limited post-penetration damage. Precision aiming is crucial.

### APFSDS (Armor-Piercing Fin-Stabilized Discarding Sabot)

**How It Works**: Modern kinetic energy penetrator. Very high velocity, excellent penetration, fin-stabilized for accuracy.

**Range Behavior**: Excellent range performance. Penetration remains high at long distances.

**Angle Sensitivity**: Less sensitive to angles than older kinetic rounds, but still affected.

**Ideal Targets**: All armored targets, especially at top tier. Standard ammunition for modern MBTs.

**Common Mistakes**:
- Expecting high post-penetration damage (it's moderate)
- Not aiming for critical areas
- Wasting on light vehicles

> **Meta Insight:** APFSDS is the standard at top tier. It's reliable but requires good aim for maximum effectiveness.

### HE (High-Explosive)

**How It Works**: Explosive shell that damages through overpressure and fragments.

**Range Behavior**: Consistent damage regardless of range (within effective range).

**Angle Sensitivity**: Not affected by armor angles in the same way. Can damage through thin armor.

**Ideal Targets**: Lightly armored vehicles, open-top vehicles, exposed crew.

**Common Mistakes**:
- Using against heavily armored vehicles
- Not using against open-top vehicles
- Expecting to penetrate thick armor

> **Pro Tip:** HE is excellent against open-top vehicles and light armor. Don't use it against heavy armor.

### HEAT (High-Explosive Anti-Tank)

**How It Works**: Chemical energy round. Uses shaped charge to penetrate armor regardless of thickness.

**Range Behavior**: Consistent penetration regardless of range (within effective range).

**Angle Sensitivity**: Sensitive to angles. Angled armor can cause premature detonation or reduced effectiveness.

**Ideal Targets**: Heavily armored vehicles, vehicles with good frontal armor.

**Common Mistakes**:
- Using against angled armor
- Using against spaced armor or ERA
- Expecting good post-penetration damage

> **Warning:** HEAT is countered by spaced armor and ERA. Don't use it against vehicles with these defenses.

### HESH (High-Explosive Squash Head)

**How It Works**: Explosive shell that "squashes" against armor, then detonates. Creates spall on the inside.

**Range Behavior**: Consistent effectiveness regardless of range.

**Angle Sensitivity**: Very sensitive to angles. Angled armor significantly reduces effectiveness.

**Ideal Targets**: Flat armor, especially on top of vehicles, lightly armored vehicles.

**Common Mistakes**:
- Using against angled armor
- Using at wrong angles
- Expecting consistent results

> **Technical Insight:** HESH is situational but powerful when used correctly. It requires flat armor to be effective.

### ATGM (Anti-Tank Guided Missile)

**How It Works**: Guided missile that can be wire-guided, laser-guided, or fire-and-forget.

**Range Behavior**: Effective at long ranges. Some types maintain effectiveness at extreme distances.

**Angle Sensitivity**: Generally less sensitive to angles than HEAT, but still affected.

**Ideal Targets**: Heavily armored vehicles, long-range engagements, vehicles with strong frontal armor.

**Common Mistakes**:
- Not maintaining guidance (wire-guided)
- Using at wrong ranges
- Not accounting for countermeasures

> **Meta Insight:** ATGMs are powerful but require skill to use effectively. They're vulnerable to countermeasures and SPAA.

## 3. Armor Types & Defenses

Understanding armor types helps you choose the right ammunition and understand why some shots work while others don't.

### RHA (Rolled Homogeneous Armor)

**Characteristics**: Standard steel armor. Consistent protection, predictable behavior.

**Effective Against**: Most kinetic rounds, some chemical rounds.

**Vulnerable To**: High-penetration rounds, chemical energy rounds.

> **Technical Insight:** RHA is the baseline. Most armor comparisons are made against RHA equivalent.

### Cast Armor

**Characteristics**: Cast steel armor. Generally less effective than RHA of same thickness.

**Effective Against**: Lower-penetration rounds.

**Vulnerable To**: High-penetration rounds, often has weakspots.

> **Pro Tip:** Cast armor is often weaker than RHA. Vehicles with cast armor may have vulnerabilities.

### Spaced Armor

**Characteristics**: Multiple layers of armor with gaps between them. Designed to defeat chemical energy rounds.

**Effective Against**: HEAT, some ATGMs, fragments.

**Vulnerable To**: Kinetic rounds (can penetrate multiple layers), high-penetration rounds.

> **Warning:** Spaced armor is effective against HEAT but vulnerable to kinetic rounds. Don't use HEAT against spaced armor.

### Composite Armor

**Characteristics**: Multiple materials layered together. More effective than equivalent thickness of steel.

**Effective Against**: Both kinetic and chemical rounds (depending on composition).

**Vulnerable To**: Very high-penetration rounds, specific ammunition types.

> **Meta Insight:** Composite armor is common at high tiers. It requires high-penetration rounds to defeat effectively.

### ERA (Explosive Reactive Armor)

**How It Works**: Explosive plates that detonate when hit, disrupting incoming projectiles.

**Effective Against**: HEAT, some ATGMs, chemical energy rounds.

**Vulnerable To**: Kinetic rounds (especially APFSDS), multiple hits in same area.

**Limitations**: 
- One-time use (each plate works once)
- Can be defeated by kinetic rounds
- Doesn't help against all ammunition types

> **Counterplay Tip:** ERA is effective against HEAT but vulnerable to kinetic rounds. Use APFSDS against ERA-protected vehicles.

### NERA (Non-Explosive Reactive Armor)

**How It Works**: Similar to ERA but without explosives. Uses material properties to disrupt projectiles.

**Effective Against**: Both kinetic and chemical rounds (depending on design).

**Vulnerable To**: Very high-penetration rounds, specific ammunition types.

> **Technical Insight:** NERA is more versatile than ERA but still has limitations. It's common on modern vehicles.

## 4. Ballistics & Gun Handling

Understanding ballistics helps you aim effectively and understand why some shots hit while others miss.

### Muzzle Velocity vs Arc

**High Velocity**: Flat trajectory, easier to aim, less drop over distance.

**Low Velocity**: Arcing trajectory, harder to aim at range, more drop.

**Impact on Gameplay**:
- High velocity: Better for long-range, moving targets
- Low velocity: Better for indirect fire, but harder to use

> **Pro Tip:** Higher velocity makes aiming easier. Prioritize velocity when choosing between similar vehicles.

### Distance and Penetration Loss

**General Rule**: Penetration decreases with distance due to velocity loss.

**Rate of Loss**: Varies by shell type:
- AP/APHE: Significant loss
- APCR: Very significant loss
- APDS/APFSDS: Moderate loss
- HEAT: Minimal loss (chemical energy)

**Practical Impact**: Engage at ranges where your ammunition is effective. Don't engage at extreme ranges with low-velocity rounds.

> **Warning:** Don't engage at ranges where your penetration is marginal. You'll waste shots and expose yourself.

### Dispersion

**What It Is**: Random spread of shots around the aim point. Even perfect aim has some randomness.

**Factors Affecting Dispersion**:
- Gun quality
- Vehicle movement
- Crew skills
- Range

**Impact**: At long range, dispersion can cause misses even with good aim. Account for this when engaging.

> **Technical Insight:** Dispersion is why long-range shots sometimes miss. It's a game mechanic, not just player error.

### Barrel Length

**Impact**: Longer barrels generally mean higher velocity and better accuracy.

**Trade-offs**: Longer barrels can be more vulnerable to damage and may limit depression angles.

**Practical Consideration**: Barrel length affects vehicle design and gameplay. Longer barrels are generally better for long-range combat.

### How This Affects Gameplay

**Sniping**: 
- High velocity helps
- Low dispersion helps
- Good penetration at range helps

**Brawling**:
- Fast reload helps more than velocity
- Dispersion matters less at close range
- Post-penetration damage matters more

**Moving Shots**:
- Stabilizers help significantly
- High velocity helps
- Dispersion increases with movement

> **Meta Insight:** Different engagement ranges require different characteristics. Choose vehicles and ammunition for your intended playstyle.

## 5. Fire Control & Sighting Systems

Modern fire control systems dramatically change how engagements work. Understanding these systems helps you use them effectively.

### Stabilizers (Single vs Dual-Axis)

**Single-Axis Stabilizer**: Stabilizes gun in vertical plane only. Allows firing while moving forward/backward.

**Dual-Axis Stabilizer**: Stabilizes gun in both vertical and horizontal planes. Allows firing while moving in any direction.

**Impact**: 
- Allows firing on the move
- Reduces need to stop and aim
- Enables aggressive playstyles
- Makes moving targets easier to hit

> **Pro Tip:** Stabilizers are game-changers. Use them to fire while repositioning, giving you a significant advantage.

### Laser Rangefinders

**How It Works**: Measures distance to target automatically, adjusting aim point.

**Impact**:
- Eliminates guesswork in range estimation
- Improves accuracy at long range
- Faster target engagement
- More consistent first-shot hits

**Usage**: Activate rangefinder, wait for measurement, fire. Simple but effective.

> **Technical Insight:** Laser rangefinders make long-range engagements much easier. Use them whenever available.

### Thermals vs NVD (Night Vision Devices)

**Thermal Imaging**: Detects heat signatures. Can see through smoke, foliage, and some cover.

**NVD**: Amplifies available light. Better in darkness but limited by conditions.

**Impact**:
- Thermal: Game-changing for target detection
- NVD: Helpful but less powerful
- Both: Provide advantages in low-visibility conditions

**Tactical Use**:
- Use thermals to spot enemies through cover
- Use NVD in night battles
- Both help with target identification

> **Meta Insight:** Thermal imaging is one of the most powerful systems in the game. It dramatically changes how you play.

### Commander's Independent Sight

**How It Works**: Commander can aim and fire independently of gunner, or designate targets.

**Impact**:
- Faster target acquisition
- Ability to engage multiple targets
- Better situational awareness
- Can fire while gunner is reloading (on some vehicles)

**Usage**: Use commander's sight to scan for targets while gunner aims at current target.

> **Pro Tip:** Commander's independent sight is powerful but underutilized. Use it to maintain situational awareness.

## 6. Aircraft & Heli Weapons

Aircraft weapons work differently from ground vehicle weapons. Understanding these differences helps you use CAS effectively and counter it.

### MGs vs Cannons vs High-Caliber Guns

**Machine Guns (MGs)**: 
- High rate of fire
- Good for light vehicles and aircraft
- Limited effectiveness against heavy armor

**Cannons**: 
- Good balance of rate of fire and damage
- Effective against most ground targets
- Standard for most aircraft

**High-Caliber Guns**: 
- High damage per shot
- Lower rate of fire
- Effective against heavy armor
- Require good aim

> **Technical Insight:** Choose weapon type based on target. MGs for light targets, cannons for general use, high-caliber for heavy armor.

### Rockets

**Characteristics**: Area damage, unguided, require good aim.

**Effective Against**: Light vehicles, open-top vehicles, groups of vehicles.

**Usage**: 
- Dive attacks for accuracy
- Area suppression
- Finishing damaged targets

**Limitations**: 
- Require good aim
- Limited effectiveness against heavy armor
- Vulnerable to SPAA during attack runs

> **Pro Tip:** Rockets are excellent for finishing damaged targets and engaging light vehicles. Use dive attacks for accuracy.

### Bombs

**Characteristics**: High damage, area effect, require precise delivery.

**Effective Against**: All ground targets, especially stationary ones.

**Usage**:
- Dive bombing for accuracy
- Level bombing for area effect
- Timing fuse delays for penetration

**Limitations**:
- Require precise delivery
- Vulnerable during attack runs
- Limited against moving targets

> **Warning:** Bombs are powerful but risky. You're vulnerable during attack runs. Use terrain masking and approach angles.

### AAMs (Air-to-Air Missiles)

**IR (Infrared) Missiles**: 
- Heat-seeking
- Effective against aircraft
- Require lock-on
- Can be countered by flares

**Radar Missiles**: 
- Radar-guided
- Longer range
- Require radar lock
- Can be countered by ECM

> **Meta Insight:** AAMs are powerful but have countermeasures. Use them wisely and expect countermeasures from experienced pilots.

## 7. Misunderstood Mechanics

Some game mechanics are commonly misunderstood. Understanding these correctly improves your gameplay significantly.

### Overpressure Behavior

**What Players Think**: Overpressure only works with very large HE shells.

**Reality**: Overpressure can occur with any HE shell, but effectiveness depends on:
- Shell caliber and explosive mass
- Target armor thickness
- Hit location (top armor is thinner)
- Vehicle design (open-top vehicles are vulnerable)

**How It Actually Works**: When HE explodes near or on armor, it creates pressure waves. If the pressure exceeds the armor's ability to resist it, damage occurs even without penetration. This is why HE can damage vehicles through thin top armor or near weak areas.

**Practical Application**: 
- Use HE against open-top vehicles (guaranteed overpressure)
- Large-caliber HE can damage through thin top armor
- HE near weakspots can cause damage even without direct hits
- Don't expect overpressure to work against thick armor

> **Technical Insight:** Overpressure is why large-caliber HE is effective even against armored targets. The pressure wave can damage crew and modules through thin armor areas.

### Why Non-Pens Can Still Knock Out Modules

**What Players Think**: If a shell doesn't penetrate, it does nothing.

**Reality**: Non-penetrating hits can still cause damage through:
- **Spall**: Fragments from the impact can damage external modules
- **Shock**: The impact can damage modules near the hit location
- **Overpressure**: Explosive shells create pressure waves
- **Module Damage**: External modules (tracks, gun barrel, optics) can be damaged without penetration

**How It Actually Works**: When a shell impacts armor, it creates fragments and shock waves. These can damage:
- External modules (tracks, gun barrel, optics, external fuel tanks)
- Modules near the impact point (even if inside the vehicle)
- Crew through shock (rare but possible)

**Practical Application**:
- Non-pens can still disable tracks or gun barrels
- HE shells can damage modules without penetration
- Multiple non-pens can accumulate damage
- Don't assume non-pens are useless

> **Pro Tip:** Even if you can't penetrate, you can still disable enemy modules. Aim for tracks, gun barrels, or optics. This buys you time or creates opportunities.

### Spall and Post-Penetration Behavior

**What Players Think**: Spall always travels in predictable patterns.

**Reality**: Spall behavior is complex and depends on:
- Shell type (AP creates different spall than APHE)
- Armor thickness and angle
- Hit location
- Vehicle internal layout

**How It Actually Works**: When a shell penetrates, it creates fragments (spall) that travel through the vehicle. These fragments:
- Damage crew and modules in their path
- Travel in somewhat predictable patterns (but with variation)
- Can be blocked by internal components
- Vary in effectiveness based on shell type

**Practical Application**:
- APHE creates more spall than AP
- Aim for crew compartments for maximum effect
- Understand that spall patterns vary
- Don't expect identical results from similar shots

> **Technical Insight:** Spall is why shot placement matters. Hitting crew compartments creates more effective spall than hitting empty areas.

### Armor Effectiveness vs Angle

**What Players Think**: Angling always increases armor effectiveness linearly.

**Reality**: Armor effectiveness increases with angle, but:
- There's an optimal angle (usually 30-45 degrees)
- Too much angling exposes side armor
- Different shell types respond differently to angles
- Overmatching can negate angle benefits

**How It Actually Works**: Angling increases effective armor thickness, but:
- The formula is: Effective Thickness = Actual Thickness / cos(angle)
- This means 60-degree angle doubles effective thickness
- But overmatching (caliber > 3x armor thickness) can ignore angles
- Too much angle exposes weaker side armor

**Practical Application**:
- Find your vehicle's optimal angle (usually 30-45 degrees)
- Don't over-angle (exposes side armor)
- Understand that overmatching negates angling
- Different shell types have different angle sensitivity

> **Warning:** Angling is powerful but not absolute. Overmatching and side armor exposure are real vulnerabilities. Learn your vehicle's optimal angle.

### Why Some Shots "Bounce" But Still Damage

**What Players Think**: Bounced shots do nothing.

**Reality**: Bounced shots can still cause damage through:
- **Module Damage**: External modules can be damaged by bounces
- **Shock**: The impact can damage nearby modules
- **Track Damage**: Tracks are vulnerable to bounces
- **Crew Shock**: Rare but possible

**How It Actually Works**: When a shell bounces, it still transfers energy. This energy can:
- Damage external modules (tracks, gun barrel, optics)
- Create shock waves that damage nearby components
- Sometimes cause crew injuries (rare)

**Practical Application**:
- Bounced shots can still disable tracks
- Multiple bounces can accumulate damage
- Don't assume bounces are useless
- Aim for external modules even if you expect a bounce

> **Pro Tip:** If you can't penetrate, aim for tracks or gun barrels. Even bounces can disable these modules, giving you an advantage.

## 8. SPAA & Radar Concepts

SPAA (Self-Propelled Anti-Aircraft) is crucial for team defense. Understanding how it works helps you use it effectively and survive against it.

### Gun-Based SPAA

**Characteristics**: Rapid-fire guns, good rate of fire, limited range.

**Effective Against**: Aircraft at medium range, light ground vehicles.

**Usage**:
- Lead targets appropriately
- Use tracers to guide aim
- Engage at effective range
- Sometimes engage light ground targets

**Limitations**:
- Limited range
- Requires good aim
- Vulnerable to ground fire

> **Pro Tip:** Gun SPAA requires practice to use effectively. Learn to lead targets and use tracers to guide your aim.

### Missile SPAA

**Characteristics**: Guided missiles, longer range, higher effectiveness.

**Effective Against**: Aircraft at long range, helicopters.

**Usage**:
- Lock on to targets
- Fire when in range
- Guide missiles to target
- Can engage multiple targets

**Limitations**:
- Limited ammunition
- Requires lock-on
- Can be countered by countermeasures

> **Warning:** Missile SPAA is powerful but has limited ammunition. Use it wisely and don't waste shots.

### Radar Lock and Line-of-Sight

**How It Works**: Radar detects and tracks aircraft within range and line of sight.

**Limitations**: 
- Requires line of sight
- Can be blocked by terrain
- Has maximum range
- Can be countered by low-altitude flight

**Tactical Use**:
- Position for good line of sight
- Use terrain to block enemy approach
- Coordinate with teammates
- Watch for low-flying aircraft

> **Counterplay Tip:** Aircraft can avoid radar by flying low and using terrain masking. SPAA must position to maintain line of sight.

### How Pilots Can Survive

**Techniques**:
- Fly low (below radar horizon)
- Use terrain masking
- Pop-up attacks
- Don't hover above objectives
- Use countermeasures
- Approach from unexpected angles

> **Meta Insight:** Good pilots use terrain and tactics to survive SPAA. SPAA must position and aim well to be effective.

### How SPAA Can Be Effective

**Techniques**:
- Position for good coverage
- Use terrain to block approaches
- Coordinate with teammates
- Engage at effective range
- Don't expose yourself unnecessarily
- Sometimes engage light ground targets

> **Pro Tip:** Effective SPAA play requires good positioning and awareness. Support your team by eliminating aircraft threats.

## 9. Practical Ammo Selection

Choosing the right ammunition for each situation is crucial. Here's how to select ammunition for different BR bands and situations.

### Low Tier (1.0-3.7): APHE Dominance

**Primary Ammunition**: APHE

**Why**: 
- Good penetration for the tier
- Excellent post-penetration damage
- Forgiving and effective
- Works against most targets

**Secondary Ammunition**: 
- AP for flat armor
- HE for open-top vehicles
- APCR for specific heavily armored targets

**Loadout**: Carry mostly APHE, some HE, limited APCR for specific threats.

> **Pro Tip:** At low tier, APHE is king. It's effective and forgiving. Don't overthink it.

### Mid-High Tier (4.0-7.7): APHE vs Early APDS/HEAT

**Primary Ammunition**: APHE still dominant, but APDS and HEAT appear.

**When to Use APHE**: 
- Against most targets
- When you need post-penetration damage
- Against lightly to moderately armored vehicles

**When to Use APDS**: 
- Against heavily armored targets
- At longer ranges
- When you need penetration over damage

**When to Use HEAT**: 
- Against heavily armored targets frontally
- When APHE won't penetrate
- Against specific vehicle types

**Loadout**: Mix of APHE, APDS/HEAT, and HE. Adapt based on targets.

> **Warning:** Mid-tier introduces new ammunition types. Learn when to use each. Don't stick to one type exclusively.

### Top Tier (8.0+): APFSDS as Main

**Primary Ammunition**: APFSDS (darts)

**Why**: 
- Excellent penetration
- Good range performance
- Standard for modern MBTs
- Effective against all armored targets

**Secondary Ammunition**: 
- HE or HEAT for light vehicles
- Sometimes HEAT for specific situations
- Limited HE for open-top vehicles

**Loadout**: Mostly APFSDS, some HE/HEAT for light targets, limited specialized rounds.

> **Meta Insight:** At top tier, APFSDS is standard. Carry some HE/HEAT for light vehicles, but darts are your main ammunition.

### Safety Tip: Don't Carry Full Ammo Racks

**Why**: 
- More ammo = more explosion risk
- Ammo racks are vulnerable
- You rarely need full loadouts
- Reduces survivability

**Recommended Loadouts**:
- Low tier: 20-30 rounds
- Mid tier: 25-35 rounds
- High tier: 30-40 rounds
- Top tier: 25-35 rounds

**What to Carry**:
- Mostly primary ammunition
- Some secondary ammunition for specific threats
- Limited specialized rounds

> **Warning:** Full ammo racks are dangerous. Carry only what you need. This significantly improves survivability.
